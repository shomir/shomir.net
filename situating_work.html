Guide to Situating Your Scholarly Writing

Read This First

This guide is part of my Advice for Students.

I've written this guide with a perspective that I call citation positive, focusing upon the value that citations add to a writer's work. Citations contribute to the meaning and significance of a manuscript, and a writer should feel positive motivation to use them. However, this positivity shouldn't be mistaken for optionality. It's important to remember that the absence or abuse of citations is plagiarism, which can have serious consequences.

This guide is intended students who are new to using citations and references, or need to refresh their memory. Students entering college or graduate school in the USA are sometimes unfamiliar with these concepts, especially international students, but they absolutely must know this information. Your work will be subject to the same standards (which Penn State refers to as academic integrity), regardless of whether you're aware of them.

This guide covers the basic concepts of citations and references, rather than committing to a style guide that specifies how they should look. I've linked to some style guides as examples.

Citations and References Give Value to Your Work

It's rare for scholarly work to happen in isolation. Sometimes people produce stunning results by themselves, but researchers tend to be more productive when they review existing work and then build upon it.

How do you situate your work, demonstrating that you've (1) built upon others' work and (2) you haven't tried (perhaps vainly) to reinvent the wheel? By popular agreement, authors cite and reference when they borrow ideas from other people. Ethically, it's important to give credit to your sources. It also gives value to your manuscript, which becomes integrated with the literature on the topic you've chosen to write about. Ideally you want lots of integration, with citations to lots of relevant work. You want readers to see that you've thoroughly reviewed others' contributions to a topic, building a solid foundation for your own contributions.

Key Terms

Here are some key terms. I'll explain some of them in greater detail in the next section.

A source is something that provides you with information. In academia often this is a scholarly paper, but it also might be a video, a popular news article, or an interview.

A citation is a formal indication in your writing that you borrowed an idea from others' work. It appears near the borrowed idea, often at the beginning of it or just after it. It tends to be brief, consisting of just enough information to connect it with a reference, which appears elsewhere in the manuscript. A citation always connects with a reference in your manuscript.

A reference is a detailed identification of a source that you borrowed ideas from. A reference only appears in your manuscript if at least one citation connects with it.

(Be aware that citations and references are sometimes together referred to as simply "citations" or "references". For example, in this guide I sometimes refer to them collectively as "citations". However, both citations and references must appear in a scholarly paper. One is incomplete without the other.)

A quote or quotation is text that you copy or transcribe verbatim (i.e., word-for-word) from a source. Writers use double quote marks or indented margins to distinguish quotations from their own original work. A quotation is incomplete without a citation to indicate where the text is originally from.
